Ethical implications of panpsychism

why it neither undermines nor demands veganism · IUS + asymmetry principle

“If plants are conscious, doesn’t the principle of respect for conscious beings demand avoiding harm to them just as much as to animals?” — Roelofs & Buchanan (2019, p. 3012)

This page presents the argument of Roelofs and Buchanan (2019) on the ethical implications of panpsychism for eating plants, drawing exclusively from Sections 7 and 8 of their paper. The authors defend a nuanced position: panpsychism does not force us to become plant‐vegans, nor does it make veganism irrational. The key lies in their Invisibility of Unfamiliar States (IUS) thesis and the resulting Asymmetry Principle.

🧠 1. The epistemology of other minds: IUS

Invisibility of Unfamiliar States (IUS)

“Our intuitions about consciousness in other beings are sensitive only to conscious states of types we are personally familiar with.” (p. 3009)

If a being has experiences completely alien to ours—no pain, no desire, no perception as we know it—those experiences will be invisible to our intuitive mindreading faculties. We will get no intuitive “hit” that anything is going on inside them.

— IUS introduced p. 3009, elaborated p. 3010–3011

Epistemic Atomism (EA)

“Our intuitions about consciousness in other beings arise primarily from detecting particular experiences, and only on that basis inferring the presence or absence of consciousness per se.” (p. 3011)

p. 3011

Alien beings

Creatures all of whose experiences are “alien” (maximally unfamiliar) will not register as conscious at all, because we detect no particular familiar states. (p. 3010)

⚖️ 2. The Asymmetry Principle

From IUS + EA, the authors derive a crucial epistemological asymmetry:

“Our ‘positive’ intuitions (that something is definitely or probably conscious) are generally reliable, but our ‘negative’ intuitions (that something is definitely or probably not conscious) are generally unreliable.” (p. 3009)

Why? A negative intuition could mean either (a) the being is genuinely non‑conscious, or (b) the being has alien consciousness that our faculties cannot detect. Since we have no way to distinguish these cases, negative intuitions carry little weight. Positive intuitions, by contrast, require detecting a familiar state—and that detection is trustworthy. (p. 3011)

Asymmetry Principle stated p. 3009, defended p. 3011

🌿 3. What this means for plants and animals

“If a being is undergoing pain, pleasure, fear, or striving, the fact that it has those states is something that our intuitive mindreading capacities should, with enough effort, detect. To the extent that intuition judges a given being completely unconscious, that is evidence that, even if it is in fact conscious, it does not undergo anything recognizable as suffering, seeking, or other morally significant states.” (p. 3012)

Thus:

  • Animals: We have strong positive intuitions of familiar states (pain, fear, pleasure). These are reliable. So we have good evidence that animals experience morally significant states.
  • Plants: We have no positive intuitions of familiar states. Their consciousness (if any) is likely alien. We have no evidence that they undergo suffering or anything morally significant—only the absence of evidence, which IUS explains away.
🥗 4. Three options for the panpsychist

The authors explicitly state that panpsychists have three coherent ethical options regarding plant consumption. They take no stand on which is best, but all are “already live options, already familiar positions in moral debate.” (p. 3014)

🌱 Option 1: Deny moral relevance

“They might just say ‘no’: consciousness that is not organized around the pursuit of goals, or the perception of some things as pleasant and others as unpleasant, is morally neutral to destroy, disrupt, or otherwise interfere with.” (p. 3013)

p. 3013

🔄 Option 2: Death as transformation

“In a panpsychist world, all that can happen is a change in the form of consciousness… What would normally be regarded as a destruction of consciousness (e.g. the death of an animal), is to a panpsychist a transformation from one form of consciousness to another. The practical implications of this option, far from constraining our actions more tightly, actually free us from the thought that biological death is intrinsically an evil.” (p. 3013)

p. 3013

⚖️ Option 3: All conscious beings have status, but animals’ needs outweigh

“They might say that it is morally better not to kill a plant than to kill one, but that animals’ need to sustain their richer sort of life justifies killing plants for food. The main practical implication of this option would be that large complexes of plant life, and other natural phenomena, have their own intrinsic moral value—a view already countenanced by some environmentalists.” (p. 3013–3014)

p. 3013–3014

📌 5. Conclusion: panpsychism doesn’t settle the question

“The authors take no stand on which of these three options is best: rather, they point out that all three are already live options, already familiar positions in moral debate. Panpsychism can influence how we think about these questions, but by itself it neither answers them nor renders them pointless.” (p. 3014)

The IUS framework shows that veganism remains rationally defensible even if panpsychism is true: we have reliable positive evidence for animal suffering, and no comparable evidence for plant suffering. Panpsychism adds the possibility that plants have alien consciousness, but that possibility does not generate new moral obligations because we cannot detect any morally relevant familiar states in them.
