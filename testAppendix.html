Appendix: Form and Actuality

clarifying Aristotle's concepts · with thinkers and SEP references
"Aristotle famously contends that every physical object is a compound of matter and form. This doctrine has been dubbed 'hylomorphism', a portmanteau of the Greek words for matter (hulê) and form (eidos or morphê)." [SEP: Form vs. Matter]

This appendix clarifies Aristotle's concepts of form and actuality, drawing directly from the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Form vs. Matter. It uses simple analogies and connects them to later thinkers to make the ideas clear.

🏛️ 1. The Core Idea: Hylomorphism

Aristotle's fundamental insight: every physical thing is a compound of two principles: matter (the "what it's made of") and form (the "what it is").

🏺 Aristotle

Bronze statue example: The bronze is the matter. The shape of Hercules is the form. The form is not just the shape, but the organized structure that makes it that specific statue. [SEP §1]

🌊 Contemporary metaphysics

Modern philosophers use examples like a river (water is matter, flow and path are form) or a melody (notes are matter, sequence and rhythm are form) to illustrate hylomorphism. [intro]

⚡ 2. Form as Actuality and Function

For Aristotle, form is not a static shape. It's the actuality or function of a thing. To understand a thing's form is to understand what it does and what it's for.

Concept Simple Explanation Analogy (from SEP)
Form (Actuality) The active, defining principle that makes matter into a specific thing. Its essence and purpose. The soul is the form of a living body. It's what makes a body alive, enabling functions like thinking, perceiving, growing. [De Anima]
Matter (Potential) The raw material that has the potential to become something when organized by form. The organic body is the matter of a living thing; with a soul (form), it becomes a living organism. [§1]
Final Cause The purpose or goal of a thing. Deeply connected to form in living things. A house is defined by its purpose: being a shelter. This purpose (final cause) is part of its form. [§1, Physics ii 3]

🧠 Aristotle (De Anima)

"The soul is the first actuality of a natural body that has life potentially." The soul is the form that activates the body, making it a living being. [§1]

📖 Michael Thompson

His work on "life forms" explains that to understand an organism, you must understand the kind of life it lives – its characteristic activities, development, and way of being. This "life form" is its Aristotelian form.

🔄 3. Why This Matters: The Problem of Change

Aristotle introduced form and matter to explain how things can change without something coming from nothing (ex nihilo). [Physics i 7]

Type of Change What Persists? (Matter) What is Gained/Lost? (Form) Example
Accidental Change The substance (the thing itself) An accidental property Socrates learns music (acquires the form of "musicality"). [§1]
Substantial Change The underlying matter The substance's essential form An acorn becomes an oak tree. Matter persists, but gains a new, more complex form. [§1]

📐 Irving Copi

He humorously noted the puzzle of identity over time, which hylomorphism helps solve. You are the same person (your form as a human persists) even though the matter of your body is constantly changing. [cited in SEP §1]

🧱 SEP's house example

For a house to be built, the bricks (matter) must persist. They transition from a "lack" of being a house to acquiring the form of a house. The form organizes matter into a new, unified object. [§1]

🧩 4. Levels of Matter and Form: Avoiding a Contradiction

A potential problem: If a human's form is their soul, and their matter is their body, how can the body exist before the person (e.g., as a fetus) or after (as a corpse)? Aristotle's solution: matter is a relative notion – there are layers. [§1, Ackrill 1972/73]

📦 Proximate vs. Remote Matter

  • Proximate matter: The matter something is most immediately made of. For a human, this is the living, organic body. This matter is essentially alive; a corpse is only called a body "homonymously" (by extension). [De Anima ii 1]
  • Remote/ultimate matter: The matter that makes up the proximate matter, and so on down to the four elements (earth, air, fire, water). This more basic matter can exist without the higher form.

📝 J.L. Ackrill

In his influential 1972/73 paper, he highlighted this "problem of matter," forcing scholars to think more carefully about Aristotle's layered view. [§1]

🧱 SEP's brick explanation

A brick is matter for a house, but it is also form + matter itself (clay shaped into a brick). This hierarchy shows how something can be both matter (for a higher level) and a compound of its own matter and form. [§1]

📋 5. In Summary
Aristotelian Concept Plain English Meaning Key Analogy
Form The essence, structure, function, and purpose that makes a thing what it actually is. The soul of a living thing; the definition of a house (a shelter).
Actuality The state of being real and fully realized, achieved when form organizes matter. A living, breathing animal; a fully built, functional house.
Matter The potential "stuff" that can be shaped into something. The body of an organism; the bricks and wood for a house.
Hylomorphism The theory that all things are a compound of matter and form. The idea itself, explaining how the world can have both physical stuff and meaningful organization.
🦇 6. Form vs. Actuality in the Philosophy of Mind

"The objective description of a bat (its form, its brain, its behavior) is like intelligence. The 'what it's like' is the actuality—the lived, felt experience. AI has the form but not the actuality.

A p‑zombie is exactly a being that has the form (behavior, physical structure) but no 'what it's like.' Nagel's bat argument shows why that gap matters.

The 'seemingly ethical simulacrum' has the form of wisdom but no 'what it's like' to be wise—no inner voyage, no suffering, no genuine encounter.

Nagel's bat is the perfect illustration of why form without actuality is empty."

🦇 Nagel's bat

Form: echolocation, brain structure, behavior. Actuality: what it feels like to be a bat. Nagel's conclusion: no objective description captures the subjective actuality. This is the "explanatory gap." [Nagel 1974]

🧟 P-zombie

Form: identical to a human in behavior, physics, and function. Actuality: none—no inner experience. The conceivability of p-zombies shows that form and actuality are not the same thing.

🤖 Sullins' simulacrum

Form: produces ethical-sounding outputs, appears to care, follows moral rules. Actuality: no inner life, no suffering, no genuine moral encounter. It's dangerous because we mistake the form for the actuality. [Sullins 2012]

🔗 The unifying thread

In every case—bat, p-zombie, AI simulacrum, snail—we have access to the form (structure, behavior, physical description) but not to the actuality (subjective experience). Aristotle's hylomorphism gives us the vocabulary to name this gap: form is what we can describe from outside; actuality is what it is like from inside.

📋 7. How This Links to the Midterm Questions
Question Connection via form/actuality
1. Zombie thought experiment A p-zombie has the form of a conscious being but lacks the actuality. The zombie conceivability argument exploits this gap.
2. Nagel and bats The bat's form (echolocation, brain) is accessible; its actuality ("what it's like") is not. This is the paradigm case of form without accessible actuality.
3. Schwitzgebel's definition Schwitzgebel defines consciousness as "what it's like"—the actuality. His five dimensions (entity, state, modality, width, property) are all attempts to describe forms that might correlate with actuality.
4. Aristotle (form/intelligence vs. phronesis/actuality) Intelligence is the form (the capacity, the structure). Phronesis (practical wisdom) is the actuality—the lived, experienced exercise of that capacity. This is Aristotle's own distinction.
5. Sullins' simulacrum The simulacrum has the form of wisdom (ethical outputs, apparent care) but lacks the actuality of genuine moral experience.
6. Endosymbiosis The story of Archaea and bacteria merging creates new forms of life. Whether these new forms have actuality (consciousness) is the same question as with the snail.
7. Snail consciousness We know the snail's form (60,000 neurons, tentacles, behavior). But does it have actuality? The three answers (yes/no/gong) all turn on whether form guarantees actuality.
8. Great Chain of Being + panpsychism The Great Chain assigns different forms to different beings. Panpsychism says actuality is everywhere, even in the simplest forms. Reconciling them means allowing gradations of actuality.
9. Chicken/egg (form vs actuality) The chicken embryo has the form (genetic code, potential) but not yet the actuality of living a chicken's life. The egg is potential (matter); the adult chicken is actuality (form realized).
📚 8. Hume's Completely Different Angle

The question "Does Hume come from a completely different angle?" is astute. Yes—Hume's empiricism radically departs from the Aristotelian framework that underlies form, actuality, and essence. Where Aristotle gives us a metaphysics of real definitions and essential natures, Hume gives us a psychology of impressions and custom.

"When we entertain, therefore, any suspicion that a philosophical term is employed without any meaning or idea (as is but too frequent), we need but enquire, from what impression is that supposed idea derived? And if it be impossible to assign any, this will confirm our suspicion." — David Hume, An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding
Aristotelian Approach Hume's Alternative
Substances have essences (forms) that define what they are. These essences are real, objective features of the world. We have no impression of "substance" or "essence." The idea of a continuing self or thing is a fiction, a habit of the imagination.
Form is the actuality of a thing—what makes it the kind of thing it is. The soul is the form of the body. The self is a bundle of perceptions. "I never can catch myself at any time without a perception." There is no underlying form; only a succession of mental states.
Teleology: things have purposes (final causes) built into their forms. Acorns aim to become oaks. We project purpose onto nature. "The mind has a great propensity to spread itself on external objects." We see causation and purpose because of custom, not because they're in the objects.
Matter and form are metaphysical principles that explain change and identity. We only have impressions of sensation and reflection. Metaphysical principles are meaningless if they can't be traced to an impression.

🔍 Hume's Fork

All objects of human reason are either relations of ideas (a priori, like math) or matters of fact (based on experience). Aristotelian forms and essences fall into neither category—they are meaningless.

🧩 Bundle Theory

There is no "self" or "form" underlying experience. The mind is "a bundle or collection of different perceptions, which succeed each other with inconceivable rapidity."

⚡ Causation as Custom

Aristotle's final causes (purposes) are replaced by constant conjunction and mental habit. We don't perceive causation; we infer it from repetition.

🧨 Why This Matters for the Midterm

If Hume is right, then the entire framework of "form vs. actuality" is suspect. There is no "form" of a bat to which we could compare its "actuality." There are only our impressions of bats and our inferences. Nagel's question—"what is it like to be a bat?"—becomes, for Hume, a question about whether we can have impressions of another creature's inner life. The answer: we cannot, because impressions are private. But Hume would deny that there is any "underlying form" that unifies the bat's experiences. The bat, like the human, is just a bundle of perceptions with no substantial self.

Consequences:

  • P-zombies: For Hume, the idea of a p-zombie (a being with all the form but no actuality) is based on the notion of "form" as something separate from experience. But since we have no impression of "form," the concept is meaningless.
  • Sullins' simulacrum: Hume would say we mistake our own feelings (projection) for features of the AI. We feel that it cares, so we attribute care to it—but that's just the mind "spreading itself" onto objects.
  • Snail consciousness: Hume would ask: from what impression do we derive the idea of snail consciousness? We have no impression of the snail's inner life. The question may be unanswerable because it's based on a meaningless notion of "consciousness as a thing."
  • Great Chain of Being: Hume would see this as a projection of human values onto nature. We like hierarchy, so we impose it on the world.

"The mind is a kind of theatre, where several perceptions successively make their appearance; pass, re-pass, glide away, and mingle in an infinite variety of postures and situations." — Hume, A Treatise of Human Nature

🎯 9. Why This Matters: The Central Lesson
  • Physicalism's blind spot: Physicalism tries to explain consciousness solely in terms of form—neural correlates, behavior, function. Nagel's bat shows that no amount of form-description captures the actuality.
  • P-zombies are conceivable because form and actuality can be pried apart: If you can imagine something with all the form but no actuality, then form and actuality are not identical.
  • Sullins' simulacrum is dangerous precisely because it mimics form while lacking actuality: We are tempted to treat it as a moral agent because its form deceives us—just as we might be tempted to think a bat's form tells us what it's like to be a bat.
  • The ethical stakes: If we confuse form with actuality, we might grant moral status to things that only have form (AI simulacra) and deny it to things that have actuality (snails, if they have it).
  • Schwitzgebel's dimensions are all about form: Entity, state, modality, width, property—these are structural features we can observe. But they leave the actuality mysterious. The snail is the perfect case because its form is just complex enough to tempt us to infer actuality, yet just simple enough to make us doubt.
  • Hume's challenge: If we take Hume seriously, the very distinction between form and actuality may be a product of our psychological habits, not a feature of reality. The midterm questions then become questions about how we project our inner lives onto the world—not about the world itself.
"The subjective character of experience—what it's like for the being itself—is not captured by any reductive account of its physical form. This is the central lesson of Nagel's bat, and it runs through every question on this midterm." [adapted from Nagel 1974]
