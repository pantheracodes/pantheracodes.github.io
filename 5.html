⛓️ Roelofs & Buchanan · Philosophical Studies (2019)

Great Chain of Being & Panpsychism

The structured intuition · panpsychism · how they conflict

Roelofs and Buchanan (2019) analyse the tension between panpsychism—the view that consciousness is fundamental and pervasive—and our deep-seated intuitions about which things are conscious. They identify a structured pattern they call the Great Chain of Being (GCOB) intuition and explore how this conflict might be resolved [1].

"Some philosophical theories of consciousness imply consciousness in things we would never intuitively think are conscious—most notably, panpsychism implies that consciousness is pervasive, even outside complex brains. Is this a reductio ad absurdum for such theories, or does it show that we should reject our original intuitions?" — Roelofs & Buchanan, 2019 [1]
⛓️ 1. The Great Chain of Being intuition

The GCOB intuition is not a formal theory but a pre-theoretical, structured pattern in how we think about the distribution of consciousness. It is an ordinal ranking of confidence [1][5]:

Self (oneself)
100%
Other humans
~98%
Mammals (elephants, dogs)
high
Birds, reptiles (corvids)
mod-high
Fish, insects
low-mod
Plants, trees
very low
Rocks, tables, rivers
~0%

This intuition forms a kind of "scale of nature" (scala naturae). It is not merely about intelligent behaviour—it is a core sense about the presence of an inner life, even when we understand the causal mechanisms involved [1][5].

🌌 2. What is panpsychism?

Panpsychism is the view that consciousness is fundamental and pervasive in the natural world. It holds that consciousness is not emergent only in complex brains but is a basic feature of physical reality, perhaps inherent in all matter [1].

For the purposes of their argument, Roelofs and Buchanan focus on the implication: if consciousness is fundamental and pervasive, then it is present in all or most composite entities—including rocks, tables, plants, and rivers—not just in humans and animals.
⚡ 3. How do they conflict?

The conflict is direct: panpsychism implies that many things at the bottom of the Great Chain (rocks, tables) are conscious, which directly contradicts the GCOB intuition that they are not [1]. This creates a philosophical puzzle: is this a reductio of panpsychism, or a reason to reject the intuition?

🧩 4. How could the conflict be resolved?

Roelofs and Buchanan explore several options, favouring more nuanced approaches [1][5]:

  • Reject the theory: Conclude that panpsychism is false because it clashes too severely with a deeply held intuition (the GCOB acts as a reductio).
  • Reject the intuition: Dismiss the GCOB intuition as a mere cognitive bias or folk prejudice with no philosophical weight.
  • Re-interpret / paraphrase the intuition (favoured): The intuition might be tracking something related to but different from consciousness, such as:
    • Evaluative richness: We might be ranking the richness and complexity of experience, not sheer presence/absence. A panpsychist could agree a rock's experience is so simple as to be practically negligible.
    • Limits of mindreading: Our intuitive "mindreading" system is designed to detect minds like ours. It fails to give a clear answer for radically different entities, resulting in the graded uncertainty (the GCOB) [5].
"Perhaps the intuition is actually tracking something related to but different from consciousness, such as the evaluative richness of experience, or the limits of our mindreading capacities." — Roelofs & Buchanan [1]

📋 Key insights from Roelofs & Buchanan (2019)

  • The GCOB intuition is robust and structured—it's not just a binary "yes/no" but a graded ordinal ranking [5].
  • Panpsychism's conflict with this intuition is a central challenge for the view, but not necessarily fatal.
  • Analogous conflicts arise for other theories (e.g., integrated information theory implies consciousness in simple systems) [1].
  • Nuanced approaches aim to "reconcile" rather than fully reject either side—e.g., by showing the intuition tracks something else, or by circumscribing which parts of the intuition are trustworthy [1][5].
  • They introduce the idea of "Inference from Understanding of Self" (IUS) and "Explanation by Analogy" (EA) as frameworks for understanding how we attribute consciousness to others [5].
