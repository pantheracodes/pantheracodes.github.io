🧟 Philosophical Zombies · p‑zombies

the thought experiment that challenges physicalism · multiple answers
1. Describe the zombie thought experiment
👤 ↔ 🧟
definition

Philosophical zombie

A being that is physically identical to a conscious human—same brain states, same behavior, same outputs—but has no subjective experience (no qualia, no inner “what it’s like”). When you poke it, it says “ouch!” and winces, but feels nothing.

conceivability

We can imagine it

David Chalmers argues that we can conceive of such a creature without contradiction. It behaves exactly like us, talks about consciousness, writes philosophy papers—yet inside there is darkness. No one is home.

not hollywood

Not brain‑eaters

P‑zombies are not groaning monsters. They are perfect duplicates of you, except that the “light is off” inside. They are the same in every physical respect—same memories, same speech—but lack inner experience.

the hard problem

Why is there experience at all?

The very fact that we can imagine a world of zombies (a zombie twin Earth) shows that the existence of consciousness is an extra fact beyond the physical. If the physical facts don’t force consciousness, then consciousness is not physical.

evolution & zombies

Would evolution create zombies?

If zombies are possible, then evolution could have produced beings with identical behavior but no inner life. But we are not zombies. So why did consciousness arise? The zombie argument makes the existence of consciousness look like a surplus—something physicalism can’t explain.

epistemic gap

The knowledge argument

Even if you knew every physical fact about a zombie, you wouldn’t know what it’s like to be it. The zombie hypothesis highlights an explanatory gap between physical descriptions and felt experience.

💭 In your own words: “A p‑zombie is a perfect physical copy of a person, but with no inner life—no joy, no pain, no ‘what it’s like’ to be them. It’s a being that has the form of a human (intelligence, behavior) but not the actuality of consciousness.”

2. How is it a challenge for physicalism?
⚡ → 🧠 → ❓

🔹 The core logical structure

  • 1. If physicalism is true, then all facts are determined by physical facts.
  • 2. If p‑zombies are conceivable, they are metaphysically possible.
  • 3. If they are possible, then physical facts do not determine conscious facts.
  • 4. Therefore, physicalism is false.

🔹 Why conceivability matters

Physicalists often deny step 2: they argue that what seems conceivable may not be genuinely possible (e.g. water ≠ H₂O seemed conceivable but is necessary). But Chalmers distinguishes positive conceivability—we can imagine a coherent world—and argues that for phenomenal consciousness, the link is strong.

against physicalism 1

The modal argument

If zombies are possible, then consciousness is not entailed by the physical. There is an “extra ingredient” that physicalism cannot accommodate. This pushes toward dualism or panpsychism.

against physicalism 2

The epistemic gap

Even if we knew every physical detail of a friend’s brain, we wouldn’t know whether they are a zombie or not. That gap suggests that conscious facts are not physical facts.

against physicalism 3

Failure of supervenience

Physicalism requires that the mental supervenes on the physical: no difference without a physical difference. Zombies would be a difference (mental) without any physical difference—so supervenience fails.

replies from physicalists

Denying conceivability

Some (e.g. Daniel Dennett) argue that zombies are inconceivable because consciousness just is certain functional/cognitive capacities. If you have all the capacities, you have consciousness—so the zombie idea is self‑contradictory.

replies from physicalists

Denying the link to possibility

Others (e.g. Ned Block) accept conceivability but deny it implies metaphysical possibility. Just as we can conceive of water without H₂O (but it’s impossible), zombies might be conceivable but impossible.

replies from physicalists

Type‑B materialism

Some hold that the epistemic gap is real but does not entail an ontological gap. We have two ways of knowing, but only one reality. Zombies are conceivable because of our cognitive architecture, not because they are really possible.

🧠 Type‑B Materialism

What it is: A materialist reply to the p‑zombie argument. It accepts the epistemic gap (zombies seem conceivable) but denies the ontological gap (they aren’t really possible).

⚡ The move: “Conceivability ≠ metaphysical possibility.” Just as people once conceived of water without H₂O (but it’s impossible), we can conceive of zombies—but that’s a limit of our concepts, not a fact about reality.

In your framework (form vs. actuality): Type‑B says form and actuality are the same underlying reality. We just have two ways of knowing it: from outside (form = physical description) and from inside (actuality = phenomenal concept). The gap is in us, not in the world.

✅ Accepts: Zombies are conceivable.
❌ Denies: Zombies are possible.

📖 Where it stands: Between Type‑A (deny conceivability) and dualism (affirm possibility). It saves physicalism by locating the gap in our cognitive architecture.

🧠 In your framework: P‑zombies are pure form (intelligence, behavior) without actuality (conscious experience, phronesis). The zombie argument shows that physicalism cannot guarantee actuality from form alone—exactly the problem AI faces: we can build the form of wisdom, but not its actuality.

🧬 p‑zombies · the great chain · artificial phronesis

form vs actuality

P‑zombies have the form of a human (intelligence, behavior) but not the actuality of consciousness. This mirrors AI: it has the form of wisdom (intelligence) but not the actuality (phronesis).

great chain of being

On the Chain, p‑zombies would occupy a strange rung: they behave like high‑rung beings but have the interiority of a low‑rung being. They disrupt the ladder.

Sullins’ simulacrum

The “seemingly ethical simulacrum” is a p‑zombie for ethics—it produces wise outputs but has no inner moral life, no vulnerability, no voyage.
