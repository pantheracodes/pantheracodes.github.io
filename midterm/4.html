🐌 Schwitzgebel · "Is There Something It’s Like to Be a Garden Snail?" (2020)

Five dimensions of sparseness or abundance

Schwitzgebel's taxonomy · lessons for snail consciousness
"The question of garden snail consciousness is emblematic of the more general issue of the sparseness or abundance of consciousness in the universe, on Earth, and in your own mind." [1]

Schwitzgebel identifies five partly-independent dimensions along which one might hold a sparse or abundant view of consciousness. These dimensions apply across entities, across time, and within a single conscious moment. The garden snail serves as a diagnostic case that exposes our epistemic limitations across all five dimensions [1].

📐 The five dimensions (Schwitzgebel's taxonomy)
🌍

1. Entity sparseness / abundance

Definition: How many entities in the universe possess consciousness? Few (sparse) or many (abundant)? [1]

🌵 Sparse view

Only humans and maybe a few other species are conscious. Snails, fetuses, robots, worms, group entities – almost certainly not.

🌊 Abundant view

Consciousness is widespread – possibly in all animals, maybe plants, perhaps even robots or fundamental particles.

Snail relevance: "Our question so far has been whether snails are among the entities who possess consciousness." [1]

⏰

2. State sparseness / abundance

Definition: For an entity that is sometimes conscious, how much of the time is it conscious? Always (abundant) or only rarely (sparse)? [1]

😴 Sparse view

We are often not conscious at all – during dreamless sleep, habitual activity, "flow" states. Maybe we're conscious only in rare moments of explicit self-reflection.

✨ Abundant view

Even when not awake or in REM, we have dreams or dreamlike experiences. Driving absent-mindedly, we don't blank out completely.

🧊 The refrigerator light error: We might think we're always conscious because whenever we check, we are – like thinking the fridge light is always on because it's on when we open the door. [Thomas 1999, cited in 1]
👂👁️

3. Modal sparseness / abundance

Definition: Within a conscious entity at a time, how many modalities of experience occur simultaneously? One or two (sparse) or many (abundant)? [1]

🎯 Sparse view

When thinking about a meeting, you have no auditory experience of the clock tower, no tactile experience of your feet in shoes. Those are processed non-consciously.

🌈 Abundant view

People normally have lots of experiences at once – vision, hearing, touch, proprioception – though most are peripheral and quickly forgotten.

Refrigerator light applies here too: We might think we always feel our feet in shoes because we notice when we check.

📏

4. Width sparseness / abundance

Definition: Within a conscious modality at a time, how wide is the band of experience? Narrow (sparse) or wide (abundant)? [1]

Width includes angular width (field of vision), breadth of field, bandwidth, richness of information.

🎯 Sparse view

Reading a book: you experience only the small portion you're looking at directly, not the whole page or surrounding environment.

🌅 Abundant view

You normally experience the whole page, the environment beyond, background noise while listening, proprioception of your whole body.

Examples: Do you experience your head tilt, back angle, left foot while driving? Abundant says yes; sparse says no.

🎨

5. Property sparseness / abundance

Definition: Within the experienced band of a modality, how many types of properties are phenomenally experienced? Few (sparse) or many (abundant)? [1]

📐 Sparse view

Visual experience includes only low-level properties: shape, color, orientation. Not high-level properties like "being a tree" or "being an inviting place to sit."

🌳 Abundant view

We directly experience high-level properties – we see a tree as a tree, a face as angry.

Note: This dimension partly overlaps with modal and width questions but is conceptually distinct.

"To review this taxonomy: Lots of entities might have conscious experiences, or only a few. Within conscious entities, they might be conscious almost all of the time or only rarely. In those moments of consciousness, they might have many modalities of experience at once or only a few. Within a conscious modality of an entity at a particular moment, there might be wide band of experience or only a narrow band. And within whatever band of experience is conscious in a modality in an entity at a time, there might be a wealth of experienced property types or only a few. All of these issues admit of considerable debate." [1]

🐌 Lessons for thinking about consciousness in a snail
  • The snail is a diagnostic case across all five dimensions: We cannot confidently place snails on any of these dimensions. Do they have entity consciousness? How much of the time? How many modalities? How wide? What properties? Our ignorance spans the entire taxonomy [1].
  • Refrigerator light error applies interspecies: Just as we might overestimate our own consciousness due to checking bias, we might project human-like consciousness onto snails because we observe them when they're active. But a snail at rest might be experientially blank [1].
  • Modal and width questions are even harder for snails: Do snails have olfactory experience? Tactile? Visual (they have primitive eyes)? Proprioceptive? Even if they have some experience, the bandwidth might be extremely narrow—perhaps just a single undifferentiated feeling of "something" [1].
  • Property sparseness: If snails are conscious, their experience is likely extremely property-sparse—maybe only very simple valence (attraction/aversion) without any of the rich texture of human perception [1].
  • The dimensions are independent, so the snail's profile could be mixed: Snails might be entity-conscious (they have some experience) but state-sparse (only conscious occasionally), modally sparse (only one modality at a time), width-narrow, and property-sparse. Or any combination. We have no way to decide [1].
  • Lessons for methodology: The five dimensions show that asking simply "is the snail conscious?" is too crude. The real questions are: In what way? How much? How often? With what richness? The snail forces us to refine our questions [1].
  • The "thread that unravels the sweater": The garden snail is not a minor puzzle; it exposes that our assumptions about sparseness/abundance on every dimension are just assumptions. We are in a "horrible epistemic position" regarding the distribution of consciousness [1].
"Contemplate the garden snail with sufficient care and you will discover, I think, that we human beings, in our current scientific condition, have little ground for making confident assertions about one of the most general and foundational questions of the science of consciousness: How widespread is consciousness in the universe?" [1]
