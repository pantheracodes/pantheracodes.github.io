Dimensions of Animal Consciousness

Five dimensions · consciousness profiles · evidence in elephants, cephalopods, corvids

Birch, Schnell, and Clayton (2020) propose a multidimensional framework for understanding how consciousness varies across species [1]. They reject the idea of a single scale from "less conscious" to "more conscious." Instead, each species has a distinctive consciousness profile across five independent dimensions.

"By assessing a given species against each dimension, we can construct a consciousness profile for that species. On this framework, there is no single scale along which species can be ranked as more or less conscious. Rather, each species has its own distinctive consciousness profile." — Birch et al., 2020 [1]

🔬 The five dimensions of animal consciousness (with empirical evidence)
👁️

1. Perceptual richness (p-richness)

Definition: The level of detail and complexity in an animal's conscious sensory experience. Varies by modality (vision, olfaction, etc.). [1][5]

  • Elephants: Olfactory richness is extremely high — they have the largest number of olfactory receptor genes of any species tested (around 2,000), and can distinguish human ethnic groups by scent cues [1][9].
  • Corvids: Visual richness is exceptional — flicker fusion frequency up to ~120-140 Hz (humans ~60 Hz); Eurasian jays perceive 120 Hz video as smooth [5]. New Caledonian crows have wide binocular field for tool use [5].
  • Cephalopods: Visual richness with polarization sensitivity and skin-based "sight" (light-sensitive proteins in skin) [4].
❤️

2. Evaluative richness (e-richness)

Definition: The richness of affective/emotional experience — pleasure, pain, moods, valence. [1]

  • Elephants: Strong evidence for grief, mourning, play, and emotional contagion [9].
  • Corvids: Play behavior, consolation after conflict (crows console victims of aggression), mood-congruent judgment (optimism/pessimism tests) [5].
  • Cephalopods: Pain-related responses — octopuses avoid places where they experienced pain, show conditioned place aversion; motivational trade-offs (e.g., hermit crabs) [1][9].
🔄

3. Integration at a time (synchronic unity)

Definition: The degree to which diverse experiences are bound into a unified conscious moment. [1]

  • Elephants: Strong cross-modal integration (e.g., matching olfactory and visual cues) [9].
  • Corvids: Moderate integration — hemispheric specialization but pallial connectivity allows binding; interocular transfer studies [5].
  • Cephalopods: Octopuses have distributed nervous system (2/3 neurons in arms) — possibly less synchronic unity; each arm may have semi-autonomous processing [1][4].
⏳

4. Integration across time (diachronic unity)

Definition: Binding experiences across time — episodic memory, future planning, mental time travel. [1]

  • Corvids: Scrub jays demonstrate episodic-like memory (what-where-when) and future planning — caching food for future needs, even trading off current wants [5][9]. Ravens plan for tool use and bartering [5].
  • Cephalopods: Cuttlefish show source memory (remember whether they saw or smelled prey) [9].
  • Elephants: Long-term spatial memory — remember waterholes and social associates for decades [1].
🪞

5. Self-consciousness (selfhood)

Definition: Awareness of self as distinct individual; theory of mind; mirror self-recognition. [1]

  • Corvids: Magpies pass mirror self-recognition test; scrub jays show theory of mind — they re-cache food when observed by another jay [5].
  • Elephants: Mirror self-recognition in Asian elephants; consolation behavior suggests empathy [9].
  • Cephalopods: Octopuses show contingency testing (possible self-directed behavior) but no clear mirror self-recognition yet [4].
🐘🦑🐦‍⬛ Consciousness profiles: elephants, cephalopods, corvids

🐘 Elephants

p-richness Olfactory: extremely high (2,000 olfactory genes). Visual: moderate, less detailed than primates [1].

e-richness High: grief, play, emotional contagion, mourning [9].

synchronic unity Strong integration across senses (e.g., smell+vision) [9].

diachronic unity Excellent long-term memory (waterholes, social bonds) [1].

selfhood Mirror self-recognition in Asian elephants; empathy indicators [9].

🐙 Cephalopods

p-richness Polarized vision, skin light-sensitivity; visual detail high but differs by species [4].

e-richness Pain responses, conditioned place aversion, motivational trade-offs [1].

synchronic unity Possibly lower due to distributed nervous system (arm autonomy) [1][4].

diachronic unity Cuttlefish source memory (smell vs. sight); future planning? [9]

selfhood No clear mirror recognition; contingency testing (awareness of own body?) [4].

🐦‍⬛ Corvids

p-richness High visual acuity, flicker fusion >120 Hz, wide binocular field (tool users) [5].

e-richness Play, consolation after conflict, mood bias [5].

synchronic unity Moderate: interhemispheric transfer but some specialization [5].

diachronic unity Episodic memory, future planning (caching for tomorrow), tool manufacture [5][9].

selfhood Magpies pass mirror test; scrub jays theory of mind (re-caching when observed) [5].

📋 Selected experimental evidence (from Birch et al. and follow-up studies)

  • Corvid visual consciousness: Nieder et al. (2020) recorded single neurons in crow NCL — two-stage processing: first unconscious detection, later burst correlates with conscious perception (similar to primates) [5].
  • Cephalopod pain: Hermit crabs (analogous) leave a preferred shell when shocked; motivational trade-offs indicate affective experience [9].
  • Elephant olfaction: African elephants distinguish human ethnic groups by scent alone; can follow scent trails [9].
  • Corvid future planning: Western scrub-jays cache food in specific locations where they expect to be hungry next morning; ravens barter tokens for future reward [5].
"A species might be high on perceptual richness but low on self-consciousness, or high on evaluative richness but low on diachronic unity. There is no single answer to 'which is more conscious?' – only comparisons dimension by dimension." [1][3]
