⛓️ Roelofs & Buchanan · Philosophical Studies (2018)

Panpsychism and the Great Chain of Being

How to reconcile two conflicting pictures of consciousness

Roelofs and Buchanan (2018) examine the tension between panpsychism—the view that consciousness is pervasive, even in things without complex brains—and the everyday intuition that consciousness is rare and hierarchically arranged (the Great Chain of Being intuition) [1][2]. They argue that instead of wholly rejecting either side, we can modify one or both to achieve reconciliation.

"We consider a variety of ways that the tension between this intuition and panpsychism could be resolved, ranging from complete rejection of the theory to complete dismissal of the intuition, but argue in favour of more nuanced approaches which try to reconcile the two." [2]
🔄 Two strategies for reconciliation

🌱 1. Weaken panpsychism

How can panpsychism be weakened to accommodate the Great Chain of Being intuition?

Panpsychism typically says all fundamental physical entities have some form of consciousness. To accommodate the intuition that consciousness varies dramatically in sophistication, Roelofs and Buchanan suggest several modifications [1][3]:

  • Degrees of consciousness: Allow that different entities have vastly different degrees of consciousness—from barely flickering awareness in electrons to richly unified experience in humans.
  • Qualitative poverty: Basic particles might have only the simplest possible experience (e.g., a single quality with no structure), while complex beings have richly structured phenomenal fields.
  • Disunity or fragmentation: Lower beings might lack the unity of consciousness that characterizes higher organisms; their experience might be scattered or unintegrated.
  • No self-consciousness: Fundamental particles might have experience without any self-awareness or reflection.

Result: Panpsychism retains its core claim (consciousness everywhere) but accepts that most instances are so minimal that the appearance of a hierarchy is preserved [1].

⛓️ 2. Modify the Great Chain intuition

How can the Great Chain of Being intuition be modified to accommodate panpsychism?

The traditional Great Chain ranks beings from inanimate matter to God, with consciousness appearing only at higher levels. To fit panpsychism, the intuition itself must be revised [1][3]:

  • Extend the chain downward: Accept that even the simplest things have some inner life—but maintain that their inner life is utterly minimal and unlike ours.
  • Embrace graded differences: The intuition that "stones aren't conscious" could be reinterpreted as "stones are conscious in such a negligible way that it makes no practical difference."
  • Reject absolute dichotomies: Instead of a sharp cut-off between conscious and non-conscious, accept a continuum from the simplest sentience to the richest human experience.
  • Distinguish kinds of consciousness: The Great Chain might track not just amount but type of consciousness—e.g., perceptual richness, evaluative richness, temporal integration (cf. Birch's dimensions) [4].

Result: The intuition is not discarded but refined: consciousness is still graded, but the lowest rungs are not zero—they are near-zero [1].

📐 Structured pattern of intuitions

Roelofs and Buchanan analyze the structured pattern of everyday intuitions that panpsychism conflicts with. They call this the "great chain of being intuition": the idea that reality forms a hierarchy from lifeless matter at the bottom to humans (or angels) at the top, with consciousness appearing only at a certain threshold [1][3].

⚡ The key insight

The tension is not absolute: we can relax either the theory or the intuition without destroying them. Panpsychism can admit that most consciousness is so minimal it's negligible; the Great Chain intuition can admit that what it took to be "absence" was actually "near-absence." The two then converge on a shared picture: a graded spectrum of consciousness, from barely detectable flickers to rich human experience [1][2].

🚫 Rejecting the extremes

Roelofs and Buchanan explicitly reject two extreme responses [1][2]:

  • ❌ Wholly reject panpsychism just because it conflicts with common intuitions—intuitions are fallible and can be refined.
  • ❌ Wholly dismiss the Great Chain intuition as mere prejudice—the intuition captures genuine distinctions (e.g., between a human and a rock) that any theory should respect.

Instead, they advocate for nuanced reconciliation that preserves the core of both views [2].

🧩 Example: the consciousness of a photon

Unmodified panpsychism: A photon has some kind of experience. Great Chain intuition: Photons are not conscious. Reconciled view: A photon might have an experience so utterly simple—perhaps a single, momentary, structureless flash—that it's effectively indistinguishable from nothing for all practical and moral purposes. The chain still has humans near the top and photons near the bottom, but the bottom is not zero; it's just negligible [1][3].

🌌 The reconciled picture

After modification, both panpsychism and the Great Chain intuition converge on a shared vision:

  • Consciousness is universal but graded.
  • Higher beings have richer, more unified, more self-aware consciousness.
  • Lower beings have minimal, fragmented, or extremely simple consciousness.
  • The hierarchy is a spectrum, not a series of sharp thresholds.

This preserves the spirit of the Great Chain (there is a meaningful hierarchy) while honoring the panpsychist insight (consciousness is fundamental and everywhere) [1][3].
