🧠 Schwitzgebel · Defining Consciousness

definition by example · positive and negative cases · metaphysical innocence

“Phenomenal consciousness can be conceptualized innocently enough that its existence should be accepted even by philosophers who wish to avoid dubious epistemic and metaphysical commitments such as dualism, infallibilism, privacy, inexplicability, or intrinsic simplicity. Definition by example allows us this innocence.”

— Schwitzgebel, "Phenomenal Consciousness, Defined and Defended as Innocently as I Can Manage" (2016)

📌 How does Schwitzgebel define consciousness?

❌ Not by theory

He deliberately avoids giving a theoretical definition (e.g., “consciousness = global workspace” or “consciousness = integrated information”). Such definitions beg the question against rival theories .

✅ By pointing

Instead, he defines consciousness by providing positive examples (cases that clearly involve phenomenal consciousness) and negative examples (cases that clearly do not). He then says: “the most folk‑psychologically obvious property that the positive examples share and the negative examples lack is phenomenal consciousness” .

“Here are some examples: vivid visual imagery of one’s house or apartment as viewed from the street, the auditory experience of listening to music, the vivid emotional experience of surging with anger at having been mistreated, the experience of having a song running annoyingly through your head, the experience of thinking about how best to get across town to grandma’s house during rush hour, the sudden sting of pain when you stub your toe.”

This approach is “innocent” because it doesn’t commit us to any particular metaphysics—dualism, physicalism, panpsychism, etc. It simply points to what we already (pre‑theoretically) recognize .

✅ Positive examples (conscious experiences)
sensory

seeing red

The visual experience of redness—the “what it’s like” to see a ripe tomato or a stop sign .

auditory

listening to music

The experience of hearing a melody, with its texture, rhythm, and emotional tone .

emotion

surge of anger

The vivid, embodied feeling of anger when you’ve been mistreated—not just the thought, but the felt heat .

imagery

visualizing your house

Closing your eyes and picturing your home as viewed from the street—there’s something it’s like to do that .

inner speech

earworm

Having a song stuck in your head, playing over and over—a conscious auditory experience .

pain

stubbing your toe

The sudden, sharp, unmistakable feeling of pain—not just the reflex, but the hurt .

thinking

planning a route

The experience of thinking through how to get across town during rush hour—the mental effort, the imagery, the inner speech .

dreams

dreaming

While dreaming, you have experiences—even if you later forget them .

❌ Negative examples (not conscious)
physiological

growth hormone release

Your body releases hormones while you sleep, but there’s no “what it’s like” to do so .

memory

dispositional knowledge

Knowing that Paris is the capital of France, stored in memory but not currently thought about—no experience .

intention

standing intention

Your intention to go to the store later—not something you’re constantly feeling .

reflex

pupil contraction

Your pupil contracts in bright light—a reflex, not a conscious experience .

early vision

early visual processing

Processing in V1 that never reaches awareness—no “what it’s like” .

digestion

heartbeat regulation

Autonomic processes that occur without any accompanying experience .

🧩 What the examples accomplish

They pick out the phenomenon

By listing clear cases, Schwitzgebel identifies what we’re talking about without needing a definition that would beg theoretical questions. The shared property of the positive examples (and absence in the negative ones) is phenomenal consciousness .

They leave room for inquiry

This method allows us to be “phenomenal realists” while remaining agnostic about hard cases (snails, fetuses, AI). We can agree on the paradigm cases and then investigate the borderline ones .

“I hope it seems clear that all of these examples share an obvious property – a property that is lacking in, for example, early visual processing, the signaling of growth hormone release, and heartbeat regulation (though all of those happen in the brain). This shared property is the property that philosophers refer to as being phenomenally conscious, or belonging to the stream of experience, or being part of ‘what it’s like’ to be you.”

📋 Summary: Schwitzgebel's definition by example

aspectdescription
definition methoddefinition by example (positive vs. negative cases) – not theoretical
positive examplesseeing red, listening to music, anger, visualizing, earworms, pain, planning, dreams
negative exampleshormone release, dispositional knowledge, standing intentions, reflexes, early vision, heartbeat regulation
what it avoidsdualism, physicalism, panpsychism, infallibilism, privacy, inexplicability
what it preservesthe ability to ask hard questions about borderline cases

🧠 Connection to Nagel: This is exactly Nagel's “what it’s like” notion, but made “innocent” by refusing to build in any metaphysical commitments. The positive examples are cases where there is something it’s like to be that being; the negative examples are cases where there isn’t .
