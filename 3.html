Question‑begging Theories & Antecedent Plausibility

Why theories of consciousness, applied to other species, are inevitably question‑begging

Eric Schwitzgebel (2020) argues that the question "are garden snails conscious?" (or any non‑human species) admits of three possible answers—yes, no, and denial of the question—all of which have antecedent plausibility before we apply any theory of consciousness. Crucially, they retain their plausibility after applying theories, because those theories are inevitably question‑begging when extended beyond humans [1][2].

"The question 'are garden snails conscious?' admits of three possible answers: yes, no, and denial that the question admits of a yes-or-no answer. All three answers have some antecedent plausibility, prior to the application of theories of consciousness. All three answers retain their plausibility after the application of theories of consciousness. This is because theories of consciousness, when applied to such a different species, are inevitably question-begging and rely crucially on dubious extrapolation from the introspections and verbal reports of a single species." — Schwitzgebel, "Is There Something it’s Like to be a Garden Snail?" [1]

A note on begging the question

In philosophy (correct use): “Begging the question” is a logical fallacy (petitio principii) where an argument’s conclusion is assumed in one of its premises. It offers no independent support – it’s circular.

Example: “Schwitzgebel’s dimensions show we are in a horrible epistemic position, because any theory of consciousness already assumes how widespread consciousness is.” That’s the vicious circle: the theory begs the question against rival views (sparse vs. abundant).

Colloquial (incorrect) use: “That movie begs the question: when is the sequel?” This just means “raises the question.” In philosophy, we keep the original meaning – assuming what you need to prove.

📖 Relevance: Schwitzgebel’s “three plausible answers” (yes/no/gong) are all antecedently plausible precisely because no theory can avoid begging the question about the distribution of consciousness.

🧩 2. Why are theories of consciousness inevitably question‑begging?

Schwitzgebel identifies two fundamental problems [1][5]:

  • Extrapolation from a single species: Every theory of consciousness is built primarily on human introspection and verbal reports. We have no independent access to the consciousness of other species. When we apply a theory to a snail, we are essentially guessing whether its radically different nervous system (60,000 neurons, no neocortex, different neuropeptides) instantiates the theory's criteria.
  • The vicious epistemic circle: To know whether a theory correctly attributes consciousness to snails, we would need a way to calibrate the theory against independent evidence. But the only beings whose consciousness we can independently verify (through introspection/report) are humans. So the theory is calibrated on humans and then applied to non-humans—but we have no way to check if the calibration holds. Any application to another species thus presupposes what it needs to prove: that the human‑based criteria are relevant to that species [5].
    • 🔄

    The Epistemic Circle

    To build a theory of consciousness → need to know how widespread it is (to calibrate).
    To know how widespread it is → need a well‑justified theory.
    We're stuck. The snail exposes this loop [1].

    ⚖️ 3. Antecedent vs. posterior plausibility

    📜 Antecedent plausibility

    Before applying any theory, the three answers (yes, no, gong) all seem intuitively reasonable. No theory is needed to find them plausible—they reflect different philosophical temperaments and interpretations of snail behaviour [1][9].

    🔬 Posterior plausibility

    After applying theories, the answers remain plausible. Why? Because theories don't independently decide the case—they just repackage the same antecedent intuitions. A theory that implies snails are conscious will seem plausible to someone inclined toward yes; a theory that implies they are not will seem plausible to someone inclined toward no. The theories themselves are question‑begging—they don't provide new evidence, they just reflect prior commitments [1][5].

    🤝 4. The Common Ground Problem

    Schwitzgebel also highlights the common ground problem in arguments about animal consciousness [5]:

    To make progress in philosophical argument, you need shared premises with your audience. But on questions of animal consciousness, people start so far apart—with such different antecedent intuitions—that finding enough common ground to reach a conclusion is nearly impossible. One person's starting point (snails are probably conscious) is another's reductio. Theories don't bridge this gap; they just entrench it [5].

    🐌 5. Lessons for thinking about consciousness in a snail
    • No theory can break the circle: Any theory, when applied to a snail, will either beg the question or rely on unverifiable extrapolation. The three answers remain in play.
    • Humility is required: We must acknowledge our ignorance. Confident assertions either way reflect theoretical prejudice, not evidence.
    • The snail as a "thread that unravels the sweater": The snail is not a minor puzzle—it reveals that our assumptions about the distribution of consciousness are just assumptions, and that our epistemic position is far worse than we normally admit [1].
    "Contemplate the garden snail with sufficient care and you will discover, I think, that we human beings, in our current scientific condition, have little ground for making confident assertions about one of the most general and foundational questions of the science of consciousness: How widespread is consciousness in the universe?" — Schwitzgebel [1]
