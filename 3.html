📚 Schwitzgebel · "Is There Something It’s Like to Be a Garden Snail?" (2020)

Dimensions of sparseness or abundance of consciousness

and lessons for thinking about consciousness in a snail
🌌 1. The two poles: abundant vs. sparse

Schwitzgebel frames the question of snail consciousness within a deeper cosmological puzzle: how widespread is consciousness in the universe? He introduces two opposing theoretical possibilities [1]:

🌊 abundant

consciousness is "cheap"

Consciousness might be pervasive — instantiated almost everywhere there’s a bit of interesting complexity. It could be easy to build, requiring only relatively simple organization [2].

Snail implication: Almost certainly conscious — 60,000 central neurons + complex behavior would be more than enough for simple tactile, olfactory, or gustatory experience.

🏜️ sparse

consciousness is "expensive"

Consciousness might be rare — demanding high levels of cognitive sophistication, specific biological conditions, or capacities for self-representation that may exist only in a few species [2].

Snail implication: Almost certainly not conscious — a snail would be an "intricate, fascinating machine" [1] with no inner experience, like a rock or toy robot.

⚖️ Schwitzgebel emphasizes that the truth might lie somewhere in the middle, but it is a "vast middle" — and the garden snail sits near the center of that middle, making it a perfect diagnostic case [2].

🔔 2. The three possible answers: yes, no, *gong*

Schwitzgebel argues that the question "are garden snails conscious?" admits of three distinct, antecedently plausible answers [1][3]:

✅
yes
simple tactile/olfactory experience
❌
no
experientially empty, machine-like
🔔
gong
reject the question / indeterminate
The *gong* option — Schwitzgebel borrows from The Gong Show: not all questions deserve a yes-or-no answer. Perhaps the concept of "consciousness" is broken, or the snail sits in an indeterminate middle space, or the question has erroneous presuppositions [1].
🧠 3. Lessons for thinking about consciousness in a snail
"Contemplate the garden snail with sufficient care and you will discover, I think, that we human beings, in our current scientific condition, have little ground for making confident assertions about one of the most general and foundational questions of the science of consciousness: How widespread is consciousness in the universe?" [1]
  • The vicious circle (epistemic dead end): To know how widespread consciousness is, we need a well-justified general theory. But to build that theory, we need background assumptions about how widespread consciousness already is. Before X we need Y; before Y we need X. The snail exposes this loop [1][2].
  • Extrapolation from a single species: All theories of consciousness rely essentially on introspection and verbal reports — and these come from only one species: humans. Applying them to a gastropod with 60,000 neurons, a radically different nervous system (non-bipolar neurons, different neuropeptides), and no verbal behavior requires a massive, unverifiable leap [1][3].
  • The snail's brain is a strange middle case: Garden snails have ~60,000 central neurons (more than C. elegans's 302, fewer than an ant's 250,000). They have complex learning (associative, second-order conditioning), chemoreception, touch, gravity sensing, possibly sleep — but also radically different neural architecture (ganglia, no clear axon/dendrite differentiation). This mix of complexity and difference makes confident judgment impossible [1][3].
  • The "thread that unravels the sweater": The garden snail is a diagnostic case. Pulling on this question reveals that our assumptions about sparseness/abundance are just that — assumptions. It shows that we are in a "horrible epistemic position" regarding one of the most foundational questions in philosophy and cosmology [2].
  • Antecedent plausibility becomes posterior plausibility: Yes, no, and gong all have antecedent plausibility (before applying theories). After applying theories, they retain plausibility — because theories are inevitably question-begging when applied to such different creatures. No theory can break the circle [1].

🐌 What Schwitzgebel says about snail neurobiology

"The central nervous system of the garden snail contains about 60,000 neurons... Gastropod neurons generally resemble vertebrate neurons, with a few notable differences: they usually don’t have a bipolar structure with axons on one side and dendrites on the other; input and output typically occur on both sides. Their small-molecule transmitters are the same as in vertebrates (acetylcholine, serotonin), but their larger-molecule neuropeptides are mostly different." [1]

Despite the small central count, snails have ~250,000 peripheral neurons, mostly in tentacles. They show associative learning, second-order conditioning, and unified behavioral responses. Yet none of this settles the consciousness question.

🎯 4. Conclusion: humility is required

Schwitzgebel's central lesson: we must acknowledge our ignorance. The question of snail consciousness remains genuinely open. The dimensions of sparseness/abundance are live philosophical options, and no current theory can decisively rule out any of the three answers (yes, no, gong). The snail is not a minor puzzle — it reveals the limits of our knowledge about the distribution of consciousness in the universe.
