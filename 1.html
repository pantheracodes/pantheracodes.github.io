🦇 Thomas Nagel · What is it like to be a bat?

consciousness · subjectivity · the limits of objective science

“An organism has conscious mental states if and only if there is something that it is like to be that organism—something it is like for the organism.”

— Thomas Nagel, “What Is It Like to Be a Bat?” (1974)

a) What does “what it’s like” mean?
🧠 → 💭 → 🦇
core idea

subjective character

“What it’s like” refers to the first‑person, subjective feel of experience. For any conscious being, there is a way that it feels to be that being—the qualitative character of its mental life.

example

tasting chocolate

You can describe the chemistry of chocolate, but that doesn’t capture what it’s like to taste it. The experience itself—the sweetness, the melt on your tongue—is the “what it’s like.”

contrast

rocks & thermostats

A rock has no “what it’s like.” A thermostat registers temperature but feels nothing. Consciousness is precisely this inner feel that some beings have.

Nagel’s definition

necessary & sufficient

For Nagel, an organism is conscious iff there is something it is like to be that organism. This is not a definition of consciousness—it points to what consciousness is.

first-person

irreducible

The “what it’s like” cannot be captured by third‑person, objective descriptions. It is essentially tied to a point of view.

qualia

raw feels

Philosophers often call these raw feels qualia: the redness of red, the painfulness of pain. “What it’s like” is the having of qualia.

b) Why is this relevant for understanding consciousness?

🔹 the blind spot of science

Physical science describes the world objectively—in terms of structure, function, and causal relations. But consciousness is subjective. No amount of objective data can capture “what it’s like.”

Nagel argues that any theory of consciousness that leaves out subjectivity misses the phenomenon itself. You can’t explain the feeling of pain by mapping brain regions.

🔹 the explanatory gap

The “what it’s like” creates an explanatory gap between physical processes and conscious experience. Even if we knew everything about a bat’s brain, we wouldn’t know what it’s like to be a bat.

This gap is the hard problem of consciousness (Chalmers). Nagel’s paper made this problem unavoidable.

limits of reduction

cannot be reduced

Nagel shows that consciousness cannot be reduced to physical terms without changing the subject. A reduction that leaves out “what it’s like” is not a theory of consciousness—it’s a theory of something else.

the subjective turn

philosophy of mind

Nagel’s paper helped shift philosophy of mind from behaviorism and functionalism toward taking subjective experience seriously. It made “qualia” central.

challenge to physicalism

p‑zombie precursor

If objective science can’t capture “what it’s like,” then physicalism—which says everything is physical—faces a serious problem. This paved the way for Chalmers’ zombie argument.

c) What does Nagel conclude about our ability to know bat experience?
👤 ❓ 🦇

We cannot know what it’s like to be a bat. Not because we lack data—but because we are the wrong kind of being.

the bat’s point of view

too alien

Bats perceive the world through echolocation. We can study echolocation scientifically, but we cannot feel what it’s like to experience the world that way. Our human imagination always fills in with human-like qualities.

imagination fails

we can’t project

We might imagine flying, navigating in the dark, or “seeing” with sound—but that’s still us imagining. It’s not the bat’s own experience. The bat’s subjectivity is inaccessible.

objective science can’t reach it

third‑person limits

Science can describe bat brains, bat behavior, bat evolution. But none of that tells us what it is like for the bat. The subjective character is irreducibly first‑person.

not just bats

other humans, too?

Nagel notes that we assume other humans have experiences like ours, but we can’t directly know them either. Bats just make the point vivid because they are more different from us.

conclusion for theory

must include the subjective

Any adequate theory of consciousness must acknowledge that there are facts that are accessible only from a particular point of view. A purely objective theory is impossible.

modest epistemology

limits of knowledge

Nagel doesn’t say we can know nothing about bats—we know a lot about their brains and behavior. But the inner feel of being a bat is beyond us.

🧠 Nagel’s core conclusion: “If the subjective character of experience is fully comprehensible only from one point of view, then any shift to greater objectivity—that is, less attachment to a specific viewpoint—does not take us nearer to the real nature of the phenomenon: it takes us farther away.”

🔗 how this connects to our framework

form vs actuality

The objective description of a bat (its form, its brain, its behavior) is like intelligence. The “what it’s like” is the actuality—the lived, felt experience. AI has the form but not the actuality.

p‑zombies

A p‑zombie is exactly a being that has the form (behavior, physical structure) but no “what it’s like.” Nagel’s bat argument shows why that gap matters.

Sullins’ simulacrum

The “seemingly ethical simulacrum” has the form of wisdom but no “what it’s like” to be wise—no inner voyage, no suffering, no genuine encounter.

Nagel’s bat is the perfect illustration of why form without actuality is empty. And why AI, lacking “what it’s like,” can never truly be wise.
